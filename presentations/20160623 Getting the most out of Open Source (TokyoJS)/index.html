Once upon a time, sharing code used to be the norm
The perceived value was in hardware, until Microsoft realized that software had huge potential as an industry. They were right.
The Open Source movement sought to protect the culture of sharing. The GNU Public License is one of the legal ways by which they have achieved this.
We owe a lot of the existence of Open Source today to friendly people like this guy.
And religious figures like this guy.
With all that Open Source around, being used by many of us on a daily basis, I find myself wondering why we don't contribute more. We owe so much to this movement, why aren't more people an active part of it?

Main reasons (IMHO)

  • Our debugging methods
  • Company leadership's understanding of software
The main reasons I've identified are the way we debug our code, and a lack of understanding software and engineering by the leadership of most of the companies we work at.

Our debugging methods

  • Gut response: I didn't cause that bug, that library is broken (sometimes right, usually wrong)
  • Our "solutions"
    1. Google / Stack Overflow!
    2. Workaround! (technical debt, hurray)
    3. ... or worse

Being a software developer means being a problem solver first and foremost.

If we don't solve the problem, who will?

Sadly, sometimes we forget this.
The real solution is to get to the root cause of our problem. Stock photo guy knows what's up.

Getting to the root of the problem, is quite literally an exercise in digging

  1. Is the problem in my code? (most likely)
  2. Is the problem in library/framework X? (debug it!)
  3. (Is the problem in Node.js or its dependencies? debug it!)
  4. Is the problem in the JavaScript runtime? (this actually happens)

Don't panic

Getting to the root of the problem, is quite literally an exercise in digging. At our company, I really push for people to go through these steps one by one, and not give up until the root cause of the problem has been identified. This can be scary, but it is totally worth it.

Report that bug!

  • You have a responsibility to report bugs
  • You may be wrong
  • Being wrong may identify poor documentation or bad API design
Authors want to know about your problems so they can improve the library for you, themselves and others. Keep in mind that library authors can often have tunnel vision about the product they're working on. That can make your feedback invaluable.
But my deadlines!
I understand you would be worried about your deadlines. Reporting a bug tells you nothing about when you will be able to deliver your blocked feature.
Fix that bug and make a Pull Request!
- Fancy developers are just people. - Have confidence in your own abilities. - Jump into the project with confidence, debug your butt off, you will succeed. - Because existing contributors will often gladly help you when you get stuck (they will see a potential new contributor in you).

Fixing bugs in Open Source pro-actively

  • Ensures the fix works, as it will receive proper review.
  • Will give you a public branch to continue to work from (deadline safe).
  • Become a member of a community around software in which you have a vested interest.
  • Improves debugging of your own software.
  • Improves understanding of performance bottlenecks.
  • Prevents others from wasting time.
Go through the list on screen. That last point is especially important to me. As a community, if you can save a ton of people the effort of debugging the same issue, by spending 4 hours on a pull request yourself, you will save a lot of man-hours. I call that pretty productive. "Work smart, not hard", right?
When we acknowledge that our interests align, this should be the obvious course. Our bosses may not always recognise it, but in an open source world, all developers are co-workers. We're in this together.
Company leadership's understanding of software
Dilbert is a bit tongue-in-cheek, but this is in essence what a lot of companies do in practice.

Demand the flexibility in your job to fix problems at the root

When your leaders fail to understand the real benefits of Open Source to your work, I believe your work environment has a serious flaw and you should demand better. Educate your leaders.

Now if you're lucky and you work for a company where Open Source is already valued for what it is, awesome. But if that's not the case for you, inform your leaders and push for change.

Open Source your own software

Always ask yourself:

Does sharing this really create a competitive disadvantage to us? If not, why not Open Source it?

  • Contributions at zero cost? Yes please.
  • Street cred! GitHub is a portfolio for yourself and your company.

Super psyched to contribute yet? Sweet!

Some tips

  • Most projects document how to contribute (read it!).
  • Some projects advocate a Code of Conduct. Act professionally and respect it.
  • Friendliness goes a long way, even when some others don't always seem to deserve it.
  • Stick to the style of the project you are contributing to.
- Code of Conduct: It protects you and others working on the project. - Code style: When you visit a friend’s house, you don’t start redecorating their home, right? Treat a project’s code the same, some people are spending their whole days in there.

Don't know how to start?

  • Documentation
  • Unit tests
  • Benchmarks
If you're a bit scared to get into a particular project and want to familiarize yourself with it a bit more first, these are 3 ways to add value to the project without having mastered it. Users are often better at writing documentation than the code authors themselves. And in the process, you will understand the code base more and more, and will be able to start contributing code.

Don't know where to start?

Help wanted!

  • Node.js and Electron modules that have compiled parts.
  • PhoneGap plugins which share both JS and Java/ObjC.
If you have some time to spare and want to help out, there are many libraries that mix languages which can make it hard to find people to contribute. The mix of languages can scare people away from contributing. But if you're a polyglot and are comfortable around multiple environments, this category of libraries could really use your help.

If you take away anything from this...

  • Acknowledge that we are in this together.
  • Demand the flexibility in your job to contribute fixes back to your dependencies.
  • Have the confidence to report and contribute.
